\noindent {\Large \textbf{PROBLEM 1.2}}  (Guillot, 2018, p. 21) \\\\
\indent Let $F$ be a field of characteristic $p \neq 2$. Let $a \in F^\times{\setminus}F^{\times2}$, and $K = F[\sqrt{a}]$. \\\\
\indent We do not assume that $\sqrt{-1} \in{F}$. \\\\
\\
\textbf{PART 1} \\\\
\indent Suppose there exists an extension $L/F$ with $K \subset L$. \\\\
\indent Suppose further that $L/F$ is Galois with $\textbf{Gal}(L/F) \cong C_4$, the cyclic group of order $4$. \\\\
\indent Show that there exists an $\alpha \in K$ such that $N_{K/F}(\alpha) = -1$. \\\\
\indent \emph{Hint}: try $\alpha = \dfrac{\theta - \sigma^2(\theta)}{\sigma(\theta) - \sigma^3(\theta)}$. \\\\
\\
\textbf{SOLUTION 1.2.1} \\\\
\noindent Since $\textbf{Gal}(L/F)$ is cyclic of degree $4$, we have: \\\\
\indent $\bullet$ \indent $L = F[\sqrt[4]{b}]$ for some $b \in{F^{\times}}$, and \\\\
\indent $\bullet$ \indent the order of $[b]$ in ${F^\times/F^{\times4}}$ is $4$. \\\\
Here $[b]$ is the image of $b \in{F^{\times}}$ via the group homomorphism $F^{\times} \rightarrow F^{\times}/F^{\times4}$. \\\\
This is an application of \textbf{Proposition 1.7}, part (2) in (Guillot, 2018, p. 5). \\\\
Already we can deduce that $L$ contains a primitive $4^{th}$ root of unity: \\\\
\indent By Galois theory, \emph{any} irreducible polynomial $f(x)$ over F with one root in $L$ has \emph{all} its roots in $L$. \\\\
\indent \indent By ``all" roots, we of course mean every root of $f$ in the algebraic closure $\overline{F}$. \\\\
\indent \indent See \textbf{Proposition 3.28}, part (4) in (Morandi, 1996, p.36) for details. \\\\
\indent We are free to let $b = a$ and $f(x) = x^4 - a$. The latter is irreducible over $F$: \\\\ 
\indent \indent $\bullet$ \indent $F$ is not a field of characteristic dividing the degree of $f$. \\\\
\indent \indent $\bullet$ \indent The problem states that $a$ is not a square in $F^{\times}$. \\\\
\indent \indent $\bullet$ \indent Since $a \notin F^{\times2}$, nor is it a fourth power in $F^{\times}$.\\\\ 
\indent \indent All this shows that there is no linear or quadratic factor that can be split off from $f$.\\\\
\indent \indent Such must be the case for a reducible polynomial of degree $4$. \\\\
\indent So $\sqrt[4]{a}$ is a root of $f$ lying in $L = F[\sqrt[4]{a}]$. \\\\
\indent Therefore (by \textbf{Proposition 3.28}) all other roots of $f$ lie in $L$. \\\\
\indent This includes $i\sqrt[4]{a}$, and $i = \sqrt{-1}$ is a primitive $4^{th}$ root of unity as promised. \\\\
We further investigate this field extension, as we often do, by: \\\\
\indent $\bullet$ \indent analyzing the (mercifully linear) action of $\textbf{Gal}(L/F)$ on $L$, and \\\\
\indent $\bullet$ \indent using norms to construct field elements (or to prove that they don't exist). \\\\
Taking Guillot's hint: let $\alpha$ be of the form $\dfrac{\theta - \sigma^2(\theta)}{\sigma(\theta) - \sigma^3(\theta)}$. \\\\
We will take $\theta$ to be a primitive $4^{th}$ root of unity, and $\sigma$ to be a generator of $\textbf{Gal}(L/F) \cong C_4$. \\\\
Clearly, $\alpha$ lies in $L$. Since $\sigma$ acts linearly on $L$, we have: \\\\
\indent \indent $\sigma(\alpha) = \dfrac{\sigma(\theta) - \sigma^3(\theta)}{\sigma^2(\theta) - \theta} = -1/\alpha$. $(\ast)$ \\\\
Note that the norm of $\alpha$ with respect to $L/F$ is given by $N_{L/F}(\alpha) = \alpha \, \sigma(\alpha) \, \sigma^2(\alpha)\, \sigma^3(\alpha)$. \\\\
The norm with respect to $L/K$ involves only those powers of $\sigma$ that lie in $\textbf{Gal}(L/K)\triangleleft{\textbf{Gal}(L/F)}$. \\\\
\indent Since $[L:F] = [L:K][K:F] = 4$, $[L:K]$ must be $2$. \\\\
\indent So $\textbf{Gal}(L/K)$ is a subgroup of degree $2$ in $G = \textbf{Gal}(L/F).$ \\\\
\indent The only such subgroup is defined by $\{1_G, \sigma^2\}$. \\\\
So $N_{L/K}(\alpha) = \alpha \, \sigma^2(\alpha)$, and this is just $\alpha^2$ by $(\ast)$ above. \\\\
Since the image of $N_{L/K}$ is $K$, we now know that $\alpha^2 \in K$. \\\\
This means that $\alpha \in K$, and we can ask for its norm with respect to $K/F$. \\\\
\indent The problem states that $K$ is generated as $F[\sqrt{a}].$ \\\\
\indent So every element of $K$ is of the form $x + \sqrt{a}y$ for some $x, y \in F$. \\\\
\indent We have $\textbf{Gal}(K/F) \cong C_2$, the non-identity of which sends $\sqrt{a}$ to $-\sqrt{a}$. \\\\
\indent This automorphism is just the restriction of $\sigma \in \textbf{Gal}(L/F)$  to $K$, denoted by $\sigma|_K$. \\\\
\indent \indent In other words,  $\sigma|_K$ is the preimage of conjugation in $K$ via $\dfrac{\textbf{Gal}(L/F)}{\textbf{Gal}(L/K)} \cong \textbf{Gal}(K/F)$. \\\\
\indent \indent To see this more clearly, observe that $\sigma$ cyclically permutes the roots of $f(x)$ above. \\\\
\indent \indent So $\sigma(\sqrt[4]a) = \theta \sqrt[4]{a}$. Therefore $\sigma(\sqrt[4]{a}) \, \sigma(\sqrt[4]{a}) = - \sqrt[4]{a} \, \sqrt[4]{a} = - \sqrt{a}$. \\\\
\indent \indent By the linearity of $\sigma$, the leftmost side reduces so that $\sigma(\sqrt{a}) = - \sqrt{a}$.\\\\
Finally, the norm of $\alpha$ with respect to $K/F$ is $\alpha \, \sigma|_K(\alpha)$. \\\\
By $(\ast)$ above, $\alpha \, \sigma|_K(\alpha) = N_{K/F}(\alpha) = -1$. $\square$ \\\\

\noindent \textbf{PART 2} \\\\
\indent Let $\alpha \in K$ be an element with norm $N_{K/F}(\alpha) = -1$. \\\\
\indent Show that this implies the existence of a cyclic extension $L/F$ of degree 4. \\\\
\indent \emph{Hint}: The case when $\alpha \in F$ must be treated separately. When $\alpha \notin F$, try $L = K[\sqrt{1+\alpha^2}]$. \\\\
\\
\textbf{SOLUTION 1.2.2} \\\\
\noindent The element $\alpha$ is of the form $x + \sqrt{a} y$ for some $x, y \in F$. If $\alpha \in F$: \\\\
\indent We have $y = 0$. Since $N_{K/F}(\alpha) = -1$, we also have $x^2 = -1$. \\\\
\indent This implies that $i = \sqrt{-1} \in F$. Since $F$ contains a primitive $4^{th}$ root of unity: \\\\
\indent Therefore we may apply $\emph{The Fundamental Theorem of Kummer Theory}$: \\\\ 
\indent \indent The finite subgroup $A = \dfrac{F^\times \cap K^{\times4}}{F^{\times4}} \subset F^\times/F^{\times n}$ gives rise to a Galois extension $L/K$. \\\\
\indent \indent If $L/K$ is of degree $n$, then $L$ is generated as $F[\sqrt[n]{b}]$ for some $b$ in $[b] \in F^\times/F^{\times n}$. \\\\
\indent \indent The order of $[b]$ in $F^\times/F^{\times n}$ is $n$. \\\\
\indent \indent See \textbf{Theorem 1.25} in (Guillot, 2018, p. 14) for the full theorem. \\\\
\indent It suffices to show that such an element $b$ and its equivalence class $[b]$ exist for $n = 4$. \\\\
\indent Indeed, $a$ is such an element: it is not a square and not a fourth power in $F^\times$. \\\\
\indent So the order of $[a]$ in $F^\times/F^{\times4}$ --- which divides the $|A| = 4$ --- cannot be $2$ or $1$: \\\\
\indent The order of $[a]$ must be $4$. So a cyclic extension $L/K$ of degree $4$ exists. \\\\
If $\alpha \notin F$, we take Guillot's hint and try $L = K[\sqrt{1+\alpha^2}]$. \\\\
\indent Note that any extension of degree $2$ over a field of characteristic $p \neq 2$ is Galois. \\\\
\indent Since $a \notin F^{\times2}$ and $K = F[\sqrt{a}]$, $K/F$ is Galois with $\textbf{Gal}(K/F) \cong C_2$. \\\\
\indent Let $\sigma$ be the non-identity. Since $\sigma$ acts linearly, we have \\\\
\indent \indent $N_{K/F}(1 + \alpha^2) = (1 + \alpha^2) \, \sigma(1 + \alpha^2) = (1 + \alpha^2) \, (1 + \sigma(\alpha)^2).$ \\\\
\indent Recall that $N_{K/F}(\alpha) = \alpha \, \sigma(\alpha) = -1$, and so $\alpha^2 \, \sigma(\alpha)^2 = 1$. \\\\
\indent Applying these formulae, we get $N_{K/F}(1 + \alpha^2) = (\alpha - \sigma(\alpha))^2$. \\\\
\indent Now, if $1 + \alpha^2$ itself lies in $K^{\times2}$, then $N_{K/F}(1 + \alpha^2) = N_{K/F}(\beta)^2$ for some $\beta \in K$. \\\\
\indent But $N_{K/F}(\beta) \in F$, which implies that $N_{K/F}(1 + \alpha^2)$ lies in $F^{\times2}$. \\\\
\indent This cannot be the case since $(\alpha - \sigma(\alpha))^2 = 4y^2a$  does not lie in $F^{\times2}$. \\\\
\indent \indent While $4y^2$ is a square, recall that we have assumed $a \notin F^{\times2}$. \\\\
\indent Therefore, $1 + \alpha^2$ is not a fourth power in $K^{\times}$, nor is it a square. \\\\
\indent So the order of $[1 + \alpha^2]$ in $\dfrac{K^\times \cap L^{\times4}}{K^{\times4}} = 4$ cannot be $1$ or $2$: It must be $4$. \\\\
\indent Consider the group homomorphism $g: \dfrac{F^\times \cap K^{\times4}}{F^{\times4}} \rightarrow \dfrac{K^\times \cap L^{\times4}}{K^{\times4}}$. \\\\ 
\indent \indent Since every finite subgroup of a field is cyclic, $g$ is an isomorophism. \\\\  
\indent \indent Indeed, by the uniqueness of $C_4$, we have $\dfrac{F^\times \cap K^{\times4}}{F^{\times4}} \cong C_4 \cong \dfrac{K^\times \cap L^{\times4}}{K^{\times4}}$. \\\\ 
\indent \indent This is an application of \textbf{Lemma 1.1} in (Guillot, 2018, p. 3). \\\\
\indent Since isomorphisms preserve group order, the preimage $g^{-1}([1 + \alpha^2])$ has order $4$. \\\\
\indent So the fourth root of some element of $g^{-1}([1 + \alpha^2])$ generates cyclic extension $L/F$ of degree $4$. $\square$ \\\\
\\

\noindent \textbf{PART 3} \\\\
\indent Show that the following are equivalent: \\\\
\indent \indent (a) \indent There exists an $\alpha \in K$ with $N_{K/F}(\alpha) = -1$, and \\\\
\indent \indent (b) \indent $a$ is a sum of two squares in $F$ \\\\
\\
\textbf{SOLUTION 1.2.3} \\\\
\noindent Again, note that any extension of degree $2$ over a field of characteristic $p \neq 2$ is Galois. \\\\
\indent Since $a \notin F^{\times2}$ and $K = F[\sqrt{a}]$, $K/F$ is Galois with $\textbf{Gal}(K/F) \cong C_2$. \\\\
\indent Let $\sigma$ be the non-identity, which sends $\sqrt{a}$ to $\sqrt{-a}$. \\\\
If $a = x^2 + y^2$ for some $x, y \in F$, then $\dfrac{x^2-a}{y^2} = -1$. We also have \\\\
\indent \indent $-1 =\dfrac{x + \sqrt{a}}{y} \cdot \dfrac{x - \sqrt{a}}{y} =\dfrac{x + \sqrt{a}}{y} \cdot \sigma(\dfrac{x + \sqrt{a}}{y})$. \\\\
Let $\alpha = \dfrac{x + \sqrt{a}}{y} \in K$. The rightmost side of the equation above is just $N_{K/F}(\alpha)$. \\\\
This proves that (b) $\implies$ (a). \\\\
Conversely, if $N_{K/F}(\alpha) = -1$, then $(x + \sqrt{a}y) \cdot (x - \sqrt{a}y) = x^2 - ay^2 = -1$. \\\\
We can rearrange this as $a = \left(\dfrac{1}{y}\right)^2 + \left(\dfrac{x}{y}\right)^2$, which is a sum of squares in $F$. \\\\
This proves that (a) $\implies$ (b). $\square$ \\\\
